LexAi vs Clay: Which Is Right for B2B Lead Generation?

Julien Gustinelli April 16, 2026 5 min read
← Back to Blog

Clay is genuinely impressive. If you've spent any time in B2B sales or growth circles recently, you've seen it come up — and for good reason. It connects dozens of data sources, lets you enrich leads at scale, and can run surprisingly sophisticated logic if you know how to use it.

We're not here to tear it down. But we talk to a lot of B2B teams who are evaluating Clay and wondering if it's the right fit — and the honest answer is: it depends on what you actually need. Here's how we think about it.

What Clay Is Built For

Clay is a data enrichment and workflow automation platform. At its core, it lets you take a list of companies or contacts and layer in additional data from 75+ sources — LinkedIn, Apollo, Clearbit, news APIs, job postings, and more. You can then use that enriched data to trigger actions: send to a CRM, kick off an email sequence, score a lead.

It's powerful. But it's a platform — meaning you bring the strategy, the logic, and the workflow design. Clay gives you the building blocks. You still have to be a builder.

Clay works best when:

  • You have a dedicated GTM engineer or RevOps person to set it up and maintain it
  • Your ICP is broad enough that off-the-shelf data sources cover most of your criteria
  • You want to enrich an existing list rather than generate a new one from scratch
  • You're running high-volume outreach and need to automate personalization at scale

Where Clay Falls Short

The most common issue we hear from teams who've tried Clay: it took longer than expected to set up, cost more than anticipated, and still required manual work they didn't plan for.

Clay's pricing scales with usage — credits get consumed fast when you're enriching at volume across multiple data sources. A mid-size team running a serious outbound motion can hit $500–$1,000/mo without blinking. That's not a complaint about Clay's value — it's just a reality to plan for.

More importantly: Clay is a horizontal tool. It doesn't know your business. It doesn't know that "ServiceNow Platform Owner" can mean either an internal hire or an external consultant, and that you only want the former. It doesn't know that your ideal client is a 50-200 person company in one of four specific industries that isn't already a vendor in your space. That nuance has to come from you — and building it in Clay requires real technical skill.

Clay struggles when:

  • Your ICP criteria are highly specific or require custom exclusion logic
  • You don't have a GTM engineer to own the build
  • You need a lead source, not just enrichment (Clay enriches — it doesn't generate)
  • You want a system your sales team can run themselves without touching a workflow builder

How LexAi Is Different

We don't sell a platform. We build a system — one that's scoped entirely around your ICP, your data sources, and your workflow.

That means the qualification logic, the exclusion rules, the search queries, and the review interface are all built for your specific use case. Not adapted from a template. Not configured inside a general-purpose tool. Written from scratch to do exactly what you need and nothing more.

The practical difference: when we finished the lead engine for one of our clients, their sales team could run it themselves with a single click inside a Google Sheet. No Clay account, no credits, no GTM engineer required. 35 qualified leads per batch, 90% hit rate against their ICP — ongoing, with no monthly bill tied to usage volume.

So Which One Should You Use?

Use Clay if:

You have a GTM engineer, you're running high-volume outbound, your ICP is relatively broad, and you want maximum flexibility to experiment with different data sources and workflows over time.

Use a custom build if:

Your ICP requires specific, nuanced logic that a general-purpose tool can't handle out of the box. You don't have a GTM engineer. You want your sales team to run the system themselves. And you'd rather pay once for something that works than pay monthly for something you're still figuring out.

The honest version: Clay is a great tool for the right team. If you have the technical resources to use it well, it's worth evaluating. But if you've already tried it and found the setup harder than expected — or if you're looking at it and realizing you'd need to hire someone just to run it — a custom-built engine is usually faster, cheaper over time, and ends up doing exactly what you need rather than most of what you need.

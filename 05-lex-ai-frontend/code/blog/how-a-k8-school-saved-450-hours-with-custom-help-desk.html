There's a version of this problem in almost every organization that isn't a tech company. IT support runs on emails, hallway conversations, sticky notes, and phone calls — and the person responsible for all of it spends more time managing the chaos than actually fixing things.

That was the reality for Lisa Polajenko, IT Director at St. Vincent Ferrer Catholic School, a K-8 school in the Chicago area managing 57 staff members, 430 students, and 487+ devices across a full campus. Every teacher support request came in through a different channel. There was no tracking, no prioritization, no way to spot patterns — and no data to back up budget decisions.

We built a solution in five weeks. Here's what that looked like.

The Environment

Schools are a different kind of client than a B2B SaaS company or an outbound sales team. The stakes around data are higher — student records fall under FERPA (the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act), which means any system handling school data has to be built with strict access controls, encrypted storage, and audit trails from day one. You can't retrofit compliance onto something that wasn't designed for it.

Beyond compliance, the users are teachers — not developers, not technically minded staff. Whatever we built had to be simple enough that a teacher could submit a ticket in under 60 seconds without any training, and clear enough that Lisa could see everything she needed at a glance without digging through spreadsheets.

What Wasn't Working

Before the system, IT support at St. Vincent Ferrer ran on pure informal communication. A teacher's Chromebook battery was failing — they'd send an email. A classroom display board stopped responding — they'd catch Lisa in the hallway. A device wouldn't connect to the network — they'd leave a sticky note or call.

The result was predictable: requests got lost, urgent issues sat unaddressed for days, and Lisa had no visibility into which problems were isolated incidents and which were patterns. When a batch of Chromebooks started showing the same battery failure, there was no system to catch it early — it only became obvious after the same issue had been reported a dozen times through a dozen different channels.

Budget decisions were made on memory and gut feeling, not data. There was no way to show administration that a specific device model was failing at a higher rate, or that a particular grade level was consistently underserved on tech support.

What We Built

The system has two main interfaces: a ticket submission portal for teachers, and an admin dashboard for Lisa.

For teachers: A clean, accessible form that takes under 60 seconds to complete. Name, role, issue type, brief description — done. No login friction, no technical knowledge required, keyboard navigable for accessibility. The form is branded to match the school so it feels like part of the institution, not an external tool.

For Lisa: A real-time dashboard showing every open ticket, color-coded by urgency across four tiers — Low, Normal, High, and Critical. Tickets are automatically categorized on submission, so Lisa opens the dashboard and immediately knows what needs attention today versus what can wait. Every ticket has a full resolution log, so nothing falls through the cracks and there's always a paper trail.

The pattern tracking layer is where it gets interesting. When the same device type starts generating repeated tickets — battery failures, trackpad issues, connectivity problems — the system surfaces that pattern rather than burying it in a list of individual requests. That's the difference between reactive support and proactive planning.

The Compliance Layer

Getting FERPA compliance right was the most technically demanding part of the build. The system needed role-based access controls so only authorized staff could see specific data, encrypted storage and transmission for everything in the database, a full audit trail documenting every record access, and automated compliance monitoring to catch anything that slipped outside policy.

This isn't the kind of thing you add at the end. We built the security architecture before we built anything else — the RESTful API, the validation layer, the rate limiting — all designed with compliance as a constraint from the start, not an afterthought. Five weeks end-to-end including compliance review and testing.

The Results

The numbers are straightforward. Manual triage time dropped from around 10 minutes per request to under a minute. Weekly admin overhead went from roughly 17 hours to 2 hours. Over a 40-week school year, that's 450+ hours reclaimed — time Lisa now spends on higher-value work instead of triaging emails and chasing down sticky notes.

Urgent issues that previously took 2-3 days to reach Lisa now get flagged and addressed the same day. Email volume dropped by more than 80%. And for the first time, Lisa has the data to back up budget requests — she can show exactly which devices are failing, how often, and what the downstream impact on teachers looks like.

"Since implementing our new Help Desk Work Order System, it has become an invaluable part of our technology infrastructure. It has streamlined communication, improved response time, strengthened accountability, and enhanced our ability to support teaching and learning. It has truly been a great addition and a powerful tool for data-driven decision-making."

— Lisa Polajenko, IT Director, St. Vincent Ferrer Catholic School

What This Looks Like Beyond Schools

The specifics here — FERPA, Chromebooks, a K-8 campus — are unique to St. Vincent Ferrer. But the underlying problem isn't. Any organization running IT support, facilities management, or internal operations on email and informal communication has the same gap: no tracking, no prioritization, no data.

The pattern is always the same. The problem is clear. The solution just hasn't been built yet.
